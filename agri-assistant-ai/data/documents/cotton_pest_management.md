# Cotton Pest Management

Cotton's long growing season (150-180 days) exposes it to a sequence of distinct pests
rather than one dominant threat, so management strategy has to change as the season
progresses.

## Pink bollworm (Pectinophora gossypiella)

- The most economically damaging cotton pest in most regions today, having largely
  displaced older bollworm species in Bt cotton areas because it feeds inside the boll,
  out of reach of both sprays and the Bt toxin's typical exposure window.
- Larvae bore into squares and green bolls; damage is often invisible from the outside
  until the boll is opened, showing "rosetted" flowers (a diagnostic early sign) or
  stained, damaged locules.
- Install pheromone traps (4-5 per acre) from 45 days after sowing; more than 8 moths
  per trap per night for two consecutive nights signals the start of an active
  infestation window.
- Destroy crop residue and unopened bolls immediately after the final picking — this is
  the single most effective control, since larvae overwinter in leftover bolls and
  plant debris.

## Sucking pest complex (jassids, whitefly, thrips)

- Active from early vegetative stage onward; whitefly is also the vector for cotton leaf
  curl virus, so its damage is not limited to direct sap loss.
- Jassid damage shows as a downward leaf curl with reddish-yellow margins ("hopper
  burn"); whitefly is best scouted early morning when adults are less active and easier
  to count on the underside of leaves.
- Yellow sticky traps help monitor whitefly and jassid buildup; a rising trend across
  weekly counts matters more than any single day's number.

## Practical guidance

- Avoid excess nitrogen — lush vegetative growth attracts sucking pests and delays boll
  maturity, extending the window pink bollworm has to attack.
- Intercropping or border rows of a trap crop (e.g. okra for jassids) can reduce
  pressure on the main crop without extra spraying.
- Rotate insecticide chemical classes between sprays rather than repeating the same
  active ingredient, to slow resistance buildup — a documented problem in pink bollworm
  populations.
- Scout twice weekly during peak flowering-to-boll stage (roughly 60-120 days after
  sowing); this is the window where delayed action costs the most yield.
