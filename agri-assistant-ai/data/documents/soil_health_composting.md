# Soil Health and Composting Basics

Soil health is not the same as soil fertility. A soil can test adequate for nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium and still perform poorly if its structure, organic matter, and biological activity are degraded. This document covers general principles that apply across most crops and regions.

## Signs of degraded soil structure

- Water pooling on the surface after rain instead of infiltrating, even on land that is not naturally low-lying.
- Soil that forms a hard crust after drying, requiring extra tillage to break before sowing.
- Visibly fewer earthworms or earthworm casts compared to nearby uncultivated or forest soil.
- Roots that grow sideways along a compacted layer instead of straight down — often found 15-20cm below the surface, corresponding to the depth repeatedly compacted by tillage equipment.

## Building organic matter through composting

Compost improves structure, water retention, and microbial activity in ways that mineral fertilizer alone cannot.

- **Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio**: aim for roughly 25-30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen by weight. Too much "brown" material (dry straw, crop residue) slows decomposition; too much "green" material (fresh manure, kitchen waste) produces a smelly, anaerobic pile.
- **Moisture**: the pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge — damp but not dripping. Too dry halts microbial activity; too wet excludes oxygen and produces foul-smelling anaerobic decomposition instead of proper composting.
- **Turning**: turn the pile every 1-2 weeks to reintroduce oxygen and even out decomposition. A properly maintained pile heats up to 55-65°C in the center within the first week, which is what kills weed seeds and pathogens — a pile that never heats up is not decomposing properly, usually due to insufficient nitrogen or moisture.
- **Time to maturity**: 2-4 months for a well-managed pile turned regularly; 6-12 months for an unturned passive pile. Finished compost is dark, crumbly, and smells earthy, not sour or ammonia-like.

## Practical soil-building habits

- Avoid leaving soil bare between crop cycles; a cover crop or mulch layer protects structure and feeds soil biology even when no cash crop is growing.
- Rotate deep-rooted and shallow-rooted crops to break up compacted layers naturally rather than relying solely on mechanical tillage.
- Reduce tillage frequency where possible — each pass breaks up fungal networks (mycorrhizae) that take months to rebuild and that help plant roots access water and nutrients.
- Apply compost or well-rotted manure at least once per season rather than relying only on synthetic fertilizer, since organic matter feeds the soil biology that makes nutrients available to roots in the first place.
