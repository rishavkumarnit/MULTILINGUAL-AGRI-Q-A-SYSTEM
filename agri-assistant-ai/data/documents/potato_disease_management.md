# Potato Disease Management

Potato is more disease-prone than most field crops because it is vegetatively
propagated — diseased seed tubers carry pathogens directly into the next crop, bypassing
the natural bottleneck that seed-borne crops get from true seed.

## Late blight (Phytophthora infestans)

- The most destructive potato disease; can destroy an entire field within 1-2 weeks
  under favorable conditions (cool, humid, 10-20°C with prolonged leaf wetness).
- Early symptom: irregular, water-soaked dark patches at leaf margins or tips, often
  with a pale green-yellow halo, expanding rapidly in humid weather. A white, fuzzy
  fungal growth on the underside of affected leaves in early morning is a confirming
  sign, not just discoloration on top.
- Preventive fungicide application before symptoms appear is far more effective than
  reactive treatment once lesions are visible — by the time blight is visually obvious,
  spread is often already underway.
- Avoid overhead irrigation in the evening; wet foliage overnight is the single biggest
  condition favoring infection. Irrigate in the morning so leaves dry before nightfall.

## Common scab

- Corky, rough lesions on the tuber surface caused by soil bacteria; cosmetic rather
  than yield-destroying, but reduces market grade and storage quality.
- Favored by dry, alkaline soil (pH above 5.5-6) during tuber formation — maintaining
  consistent soil moisture during this stage is the most effective non-chemical control.
- Avoid fresh manure application right before planting; it raises soil pH and scab
  incidence in the following crop.

## Practical guidance

- Always plant certified disease-free seed tubers — this alone prevents more disease
  pressure than any in-season treatment, since several major potato diseases are
  seed-transmitted.
- Hill soil up around the base of plants as they grow; exposed tubers near the surface
  are both more prone to greening (solanine buildup, toxic) and easier for blight
  spores to reach from infected foliage above.
- Destroy volunteer potato plants and cull piles between seasons — they act as a
  disease bridge (a "green bridge") carrying pathogens from one crop cycle to the next.
