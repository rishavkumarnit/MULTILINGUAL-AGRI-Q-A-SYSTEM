# Monsoon (Kharif) Crop Planning

Kharif crops are sown with the onset of the monsoon and harvested in autumn. Because sowing depends on rainfall timing rather than a fixed calendar date, planning around monsoon variability matters more than in irrigated winter cropping.

## Sowing timing

- Do not sow immediately at the first rain of the season — a single early shower followed by a dry spell can kill young seedlings before their root system is established. Wait for at least 3-4 consecutive days of good rainfall (generally 50-75mm cumulative) before sowing rain-fed kharif crops.
- If monsoon onset is delayed beyond the normal window for the region, consider switching to a shorter-duration variety of the same crop rather than sowing a full-duration variety late, since a late-sown full-duration crop may not finish before the monsoon withdraws.
- Fields with poor drainage should be sown slightly later and on raised beds or ridges, since waterlogging in the first two weeks after sowing is one of the most common causes of kharif crop failure, more common than drought in high-rainfall regions.

## Crop selection by rainfall pattern

- **Assured, well-distributed rainfall (>750mm over the season)**: rice, and other crops with high water demand throughout the season are viable.
- **Moderate, somewhat erratic rainfall (500-750mm)**: maize, sorghum, and pulses like pigeon pea tolerate short dry spells better than rice while still using the monsoon water effectively.
- **Low or highly erratic rainfall (<500mm)**: short-duration millets and drought-tolerant pulses are safer choices than water-demanding crops, since a failed rice crop under water stress produces near-zero yield, while millets can still produce a partial yield under the same stress.

## Managing mid-season dry spells

A break in the monsoon of 10-15 days during the vegetative stage is common and usually survivable with light supplemental irrigation if available. A break of the same length during flowering is far more damaging and can cut yield by half even in otherwise drought-tolerant crops, because flowering is the most water-sensitive stage across nearly all kharif crops.

- If supplemental irrigation is limited, prioritize using it during flowering over the vegetative stage.
- Mulching between rows conserves soil moisture during dry spells and is especially valuable for crops sown on raised beds.
- Avoid nitrogen top-dressing immediately before an expected dry spell, since actively growing, nitrogen-fed plants have higher water demand and wilt faster under stress than plants growing at a steadier pace.
