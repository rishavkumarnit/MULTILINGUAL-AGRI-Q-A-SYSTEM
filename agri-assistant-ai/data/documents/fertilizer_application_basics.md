# Fertilizer Application Basics: NPK Timing

Nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium behave very differently in soil, and applying all three the same way wastes fertilizer and can hurt yield. This document covers general timing principles that apply across most annual crops.

## Nitrogen (N)

Nitrogen is mobile in soil and easily lost to leaching (washed below the root zone) or volatilization (lost to the air), so it is applied in split doses rather than all at once.

- A common split is roughly one-third at sowing/basal, one-third at active vegetative growth, and one-third at a later stage such as flowering or panicle initiation, though exact splits vary by crop.
- Applying all nitrogen at sowing is one of the most common fertilizer mistakes: young seedlings cannot use that much nitrogen at once, so most of it leaches away or volatilizes before the plant needs it during peak vegetative growth.
- Nitrogen applied too late, close to maturity, delays crop maturity and can reduce grain or fruit quality even if it modestly increases vegetative growth.
- Urea applied to the soil surface without incorporation or irrigation loses a large share of its nitrogen to volatilization within 24-48 hours, especially in warm conditions; incorporate it into the soil or irrigate shortly after application.

## Phosphorus (P)

Phosphorus is nearly immobile in soil — it does not move far from where it is placed, and roots must grow to reach it.

- Apply phosphorus entirely at or before sowing, placed near the seed or root zone, since top-dressing phosphorus later in the season is largely ineffective — by the time it would reach the root zone through soil movement, the crop's early root development has already been affected.
- Phosphorus deficiency shows first as stunted growth and a dark, dull, sometimes purplish leaf color, most visible in young plants, and is easy to mistake for cold stress.

## Potassium (K)

Potassium is moderately mobile, more so than phosphorus but less than nitrogen.

- Typically applied as a basal dose at sowing, with a smaller top-up during the reproductive stage for crops with high potassium demand, such as fruiting vegetables and sugarcane.
- Potassium deficiency shows as yellowing and browning starting at leaf margins and tips, on older/lower leaves first, unlike nitrogen deficiency which shows as uniform yellowing that also starts on older leaves but does not begin at the margin specifically.
- Adequate potassium improves resistance to lodging and disease, so cutting potassium to save cost often shows up later as a disease or lodging problem rather than an obvious growth issue at the time.

## General guidance

- Split, timed application consistently outperforms a single large application of the same total nutrient amount, for nitrogen and potassium in particular.
- A soil test before the season is the most reliable way to set actual NPK rates; these timing principles apply regardless of the specific rate used.
