# Rice Pest Management: Stem Borer and Brown Planthopper

Two pests account for most preventable rice yield loss in irrigated systems: yellow stem borer and brown planthopper (BPH). They require different detection methods and different responses, and treating them the same way often makes the BPH problem worse.

## Yellow stem borer

Stem borer larvae tunnel into the stem, cutting off water and nutrient flow to the growing point.

- **Vegetative stage symptom**: "dead heart" — the central shoot of a tiller dries up and can be pulled out easily, while surrounding leaves stay green. Pull a few dead hearts to check for a larva or frass (larval waste) inside the stem to confirm stem borer rather than another cause.
- **Reproductive stage symptom**: "white ear" — the entire panicle turns white and empty because the larva cut the stem below it. Unlike dead heart, white ear cannot be treated after the fact; by the time it's visible, that tiller's yield is already lost.
- **Threshold for action**: 2 egg masses per square meter, or 5% dead hearts, during the vegetative stage. Below this threshold, natural predators (spiders, wasps) usually keep populations in check and spraying does more harm than good by killing those predators.
- **Timing**: if action is warranted, treat within 2-3 days of egg hatching, before larvae bore into the stem — once inside the stem, most contact insecticides can no longer reach them.

## Brown planthopper (BPH)

BPH feeds at the base of the plant by sucking sap, causing a distinctive circular patch of plants to turn orange-brown and collapse, known as "hopper burn." Unlike stem borer, hopper burn can spread rapidly across an entire field within a week once it starts.

- **Detection**: tap plants at the base over a basin of water — adult and nymph BPH will fall and float, making counting straightforward. More than 10-15 hoppers per hill at tillering, or 20+ per hill after tillering, warrants action.
- **Key mistake to avoid**: broad-spectrum insecticides applied for stem borer or leaf pests often kill BPH's natural predators (mirid bugs, spiders) without controlling BPH itself, which can trigger a BPH outbreak 1-2 weeks later. This is called "resurgence" and is one of the most common causes of late-season BPH flare-ups in fields that were sprayed earlier for an unrelated pest.
- **Cultural controls**: avoid excess nitrogen, which produces dense, succulent growth that BPH prefers; maintain 7-10cm spacing between plants rather than dense transplanting; periodic field drainage for 2-3 days disrupts BPH breeding, which requires standing water at the plant base.
- **If chemical control is needed**: choose a selective hopper-specific product rather than a broad-spectrum insecticide, and direct the spray at the base of the plant, not the canopy, since that is where BPH feeds.
