# Wheat Irrigation Scheduling

Wheat needs carefully timed irrigation more than it needs frequent irrigation. Over-watering wastes water, encourages lodging (stems falling over), and increases the risk of fungal disease at the crown. Under-watering at the wrong growth stage can cut yield sharply even if the total seasonal water applied looks adequate.

## Critical growth stages

The most water-sensitive stages of wheat are, in order of importance:

1. **Crown root initiation (18-21 days after sowing)** — the single most critical irrigation. Missing this one can permanently reduce the plant's root system and cap yield for the rest of the season, regardless of later watering.
2. **Tillering (40-45 days after sowing)** — supports the number of productive stems per plant.
3. **Late jointing / booting (60-65 days after sowing)** — supports spike size.
4. **Flowering (80-85 days after sowing)** — pollen viability drops sharply under water stress at this stage.
5. **Grain filling / milk stage (100-110 days after sowing)** — this determines final grain weight. Water stress here produces shriveled, lightweight grain even if the plant otherwise looks healthy. Irrigating during grain filling, roughly two to three weeks after flowering, is what fills out the grain and should not be skipped even if the crop looks green and vigorous.

## Practical guidance

- Light, frequent irrigation is worse than fewer, deeper irrigations timed to the stages above — deep watering encourages deeper rooting.
- Avoid irrigating within 3-4 days of a forecast rain event of more than 15mm.
- On heavier clay soils, extend the interval between irrigations by 3-5 days compared to sandy loam, since clay retains moisture longer.
- Visible wilting in wheat is a late warning sign, not an early one — by the time leaves show stress, the crown root stage window may already be missed.
- If only one irrigation is possible due to water scarcity, prioritize the crown root initiation stage over all others.

## Signs of irrigation problems

- Yellowing lower leaves with healthy upper leaves usually indicates waterlogging, not drought stress.
- Blue-green, dull leaf color and leaf rolling during the day indicate drought stress and call for irrigation within 2-3 days.
- Lodging after a wind event is often linked to excess nitrogen combined with over-irrigation near maturity, not wind strength alone.
