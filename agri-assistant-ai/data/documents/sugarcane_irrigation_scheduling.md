# Sugarcane Irrigation Scheduling

Sugarcane's long duration (10-18 months depending on variety and region) means its
water need changes substantially across distinct growth phases, unlike shorter-season
crops with one or two critical windows.

## Growth-phase water demand

1. **Germination (0-45 days)** — light, frequent irrigation to keep the top soil layer
   moist without waterlogging; setts (seed pieces) rot easily in standing water at this
   stage.
2. **Tillering (45-120 days)** — moderate, regular irrigation; this phase sets the
   number of productive stalks per clump, so prolonged stress here permanently reduces
   the stalk count the field can produce.
3. **Grand growth phase (120-270 days)** — the highest water demand of the whole crop
   cycle, since this is when the majority of stalk height and biomass accumulates.
   Irrigation intervals should be shortest during this phase.
4. **Maturation (last 6-8 weeks before harvest)** — irrigation should be deliberately
   reduced or stopped. Continued heavy irrigation this late lowers sucrose
   concentration in the stalk even though the plant looks unaffected.

## Practical guidance

- Alternate furrow irrigation (wetting alternate furrows each cycle rather than all of
  them) can cut water use meaningfully with little yield penalty during the tillering
  and early grand growth phases.
- Trash mulching (spreading dried cane leaves between rows) reduces evaporation and can
  extend the safe interval between irrigations, especially useful during the
  high-demand grand growth phase.
- Waterlogging for more than 2-3 days at any stage promotes root rot and encourages
  lodging later in the season — ensure field drainage before the monsoon-heavy months
  rather than after standing water is already visible.
- Do not irrigate immediately before a planned harvest; the sucrose-dilution effect from
  late-stage irrigation directly reduces the crop's market/recovery value even though
  stalk weight may look higher.

## Signs of irrigation problems

- Leaf rolling and a dull, bluish-green tint during the hottest part of the day
  indicates water stress and calls for irrigation within 1-2 days, especially during
  the grand growth phase.
- Yellowing of lower leaves combined with a foul smell near the root zone points to
  waterlogging-related root rot, not nutrient deficiency.
