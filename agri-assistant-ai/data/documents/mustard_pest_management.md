# Mustard Pest Management

Mustard (sarson/rai) is most vulnerable to sap-sucking pests during vegetative growth
and early flowering. Losses from a heavy, untreated aphid outbreak can exceed 30-40% of
yield, mainly by weakening the plant before pod formation rather than damaging the pods
directly.

## Mustard aphid (Lipaphis erysimi)

- The single most damaging mustard pest. Colonies form on the underside of leaves,
  tender shoots, and inflorescences, sucking sap and secreting honeydew that promotes
  sooty mould.
- Peak risk window is 40-70 days after sowing, coinciding with flowering — this is when
  economic damage is highest, not just population size.
- Economic threshold: treat when aphids cover more than 50% of the central shoot length
  (roughly 1 aphid per cm of shoot) on 50%+ of plants sampled.
- Cool, cloudy weather (15-20°C) favors rapid aphid buildup; a sunny spell with
  temperatures above 25°C naturally suppresses populations.

## Practical guidance

- Early sowing (within the recommended window for the region) lets the crop cross the
  vulnerable flowering stage before aphid populations peak later in the season.
- Yellow sticky traps installed at crop-canopy height give an early warning before
  visible colonies form.
- Conserve natural predators (ladybird beetles, syrphid fly larvae) by avoiding
  unnecessary broad-spectrum spraying — they can suppress moderate infestations on
  their own.
- If chemical control is needed, target the shoot tips and undersides of leaves
  directly, since aphids cluster there and a canopy-only spray under-doses them.
- Avoid spraying during flowering hours (late morning to afternoon) when pollinators are
  active; spray early morning or evening instead.

## Other common pests

- **Painted bug (Bagrada hilaris)**: attacks seedlings and can kill young plants
  outright; most damaging in the first 2-3 weeks after emergence.
- **Sawfly larvae**: chew leaf margins in a characteristic notched pattern shortly after
  germination; rarely economically damaging unless seedling stands are already thin.

## Signs to distinguish from nutrient stress

- Aphid damage shows as curled, yellowing shoot tips with visible insects or honeydew
  residue — nutrient deficiency yellowing is more uniform across the leaf and lacks the
  sticky residue or curling concentrated at growing points.
